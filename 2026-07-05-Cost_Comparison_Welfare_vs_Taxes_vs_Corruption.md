- **Welfare** is government spending. If you include programs like Social Security and Medicare, spending is much higher, but those are generally classified separately as social insurance rather than welfare.
- **Fraud** is surprisingly expensive. The largest recent spike occurred during COVID-19 relief programs.
- **Corruption** is difficult to quantify because much of it is hidden. Direct bribery and embezzlement are much smaller than welfare spending, but indirect costs—such as inefficient contracting, favoritism, regulatory capture, and reduced economic productivity—can be much larger, though economists disagree on how to estimate them.

If you're asking **which is largest**:

1. **Taxes** (government revenue): about **$5 trillion** annually.
2. **Welfare spending** (means-tested): about **$1 trillion** annually.
3. **Fraud losses**: roughly **$200–500+ billion** annually, depending on methodology.
4. **Direct corruption losses**: generally estimated below fraud, though indirect economic costs are much harder to measure.

One interesting point is that while fraud is smaller than total welfare spending, it is still enormous in absolute terms. Recovering even a fraction of fraudulent payments could save taxpayers tens of billions of dollars each year, but eliminating fraud entirely is generally considered unrealistic because some level of abuse exists in nearly every large public and private system.
